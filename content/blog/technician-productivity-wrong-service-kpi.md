### Key Takeaways

- Technician productivity is a lagging indicator of advisor close rates, not a measure of shop-floor effort or skill.
- Bays sit idle for hours waiting on customer approvals after advisors leave voicemails instead of reaching customers live.
- Three no-cost advisor behaviors (live answer, one-hour callback, detailed diagnostic walkthrough) move the scoreboard more than any tech bonus.
- Operationally, service managers should station themselves at the advisor desk during the 10 a.m. callback window, not the back shop.
- Strategically, dealers running wrench-side incentives on a broken approval pipeline are paying technicians to absorb advisor failures and lose their best ones.
- Expect 2026 fixed ops scorecards to shift toward approved-hours-per-RO and advisor contact rates as the leading KPIs.

## What One Walser Hyundai GM Says Dealers Are Measuring Wrong

Walk into almost any franchise store in 2026 and the fixed ops scorecard still leads with one line: billed hours per tech. Erikka Tiffani, who runs Walser Hyundai Brooklyn Park, sat down with Car Dealership Guy and made the case for retiring that habit. Her take, in plain terms: bay output is a consequence, not a cause. What actually moves it is whether advisors are closing customers on recommended repairs. Dealer principals hammering on the tech scoreboard are pressing a lever that isn't connected to the thing they want to move ([according to Visquanta](https://www.visquanta.com/internal/operator-seed/most-dealers-measure-service-department-performance-through-)).

Having sold cars on the floor myself, the pattern we see across our rooftops is this: Tiffani's reframe inverts how fixed ops directors think about their scorecard. If technicians cannot produce hours on jobs advisors never got greenlit, the productivity number is a report card on the front of the [service drive](https://www.visquanta.com/service-drive), not the back. The bay is not the bottleneck. The conversation at the advisor desk is. That shifts where a dealer principal should be spending their Monday morning attention.

Walk any franchise rooftop's service drive at 9 a.m. and you'll see technicians already working their first RO. They are not idle. They are waiting on approvals the advisor team has not closed on the phone. The GM is measuring bay output. The real constraint is whether the customer on line two said yes to the brake job.

## What Is Approved Work, and Why Does It Drive the Service Drive?

### Approved Work, Defined

Approved work is the volume of diagnosed ROs a customer has authorized the store to perform, measured in labor hours. It is the input that feeds technician productivity - not a synonym for it. Every hour a technician books comes from a job an advisor first sold on the phone or at the service drive counter.

Having sold cars on the floor myself, the pattern we see across fixed ops scorecards is the same every time: order of operations matters. A tech cannot turn a wrench on a repair that was never sold at the advisor desk. That means the productivity report is really grading the phone work and the walk-around, not the shop floor. Most fixed ops directors won't write that diagnosis. But if the approval pipeline is dry on Tuesday, Friday's hours-per-RO is dry too. No spiff plan aimed at the back of the shop reverses that. The sale at the advisor desk gates everything under the hood.

Here's what actually happens when approved work is the constraint:

- A customer drops off a vehicle at 7:30 a.m. with a check-engine light
- The technician diagnoses the issue by 9:15 a.m. and writes up three recommended repairs
- The advisor calls the customer at 10:00 a.m. and leaves a [voicemail](https://www.visquanta.com/blog/why-your-service-department-needs-24-7-call-answering-not-just-voicemail)
- The customer does not call back until 3:00 p.m.
- The bay sits on a half-torn-down vehicle for five hours, waiting on approval
- Every other RO in that technician's queue slides

The productivity number at the end of that day looks bad. The technician did not cause it.

| Time | Event | Status of Bay |

| 7:30 a.m. | Customer drops off vehicle with check-engine light | Active |

## Why Are Dealers Still Squeezing Technicians Who Already Have Full Bays?

So why do dealers keep pushing incentive programs aimed at technicians who already have full bays? Part of it is inertia. The productivity report is easy to pull from the DMS. Wrench-side bonuses are a lever dealer principals have used for thirty years. Here's the disconnect Tiffani names: when you're squeezing more output from technicians who already have full bays, you're optimizing a constraint that isn't real.

The actual lever sits inside the conversation that decides whether work gets approved. That conversation happens at the advisor desk and, more often, on the phone. A technician bonus plan does not touch it. A service manager standing behind the advisor team during the 10 a.m. callback window does. Fixed ops leaders who move their attention from the bay to the advisor desk usually see the scoreboard follow.

There is also a cultural cost to getting this wrong. When technicians see their productivity number punished for advisor delays they cannot control, the best ones leave. A group that runs a wrench-side incentive program on top of a broken approval pipeline is effectively paying its shop to absorb the advisor team's dropped calls. That is not a labor strategy. That is a [retention](https://www.visquanta.com/blog/whats-killing-retention-in-your-service-department-missed-calls) problem in slow motion.

### Wrench-Side Lever vs. Advisor-Desk Lever

| Lever | Touches Approved Work? | Typical Cost | Impact on Scoreboard |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Technician flat-rate bonus | No | High | Minimal when bays are already full |
| Menu upsell script | Partial | Low | Marginal |
| Live phone answer on inbound | Yes | Zero | High |
| One-hour voicemail callback | Yes | Zero | High |
| Detailed diagnostic walkthrough | Yes | Four extra minutes per call | Highest close-rate lift |


## How Do Service Advisors Actually Move the Productivity Number?

Tiffani gets concrete about where the real gains hide. An advisor who answers the phone live, calls a customer back within the hour, and spends real time explaining what the scan tool found will lift the scoreboard further than any shop-floor bonus has. Three habits. None of them cost the dealer money. All of them happen before the tech gets a signed RO back. That is the whole argument in one paragraph: the productivity lever is a desk-and-phone lever, not a wrench lever.

### The Three Advisor Behaviors That Move Service Productivity

1. Picking up inbound calls instead of sending them to voicemail
2. Returning voicemails inside one hour of receipt
3. Walking customers through diagnostic findings in detail, not just quoting a price

The third one is the quiet winner. Customers who understand why a repair matters approve it. Customers who get a clipped price quote say they need to think about it and never call back. The advisor who takes an extra four minutes on the diagnostic walkthrough closes more approved work than the advisor who takes three calls in the same window.

Notice what is absent from Tiffani's list. No menu upsell scripts. No technician flat-rate hours. No warranty claim speed. The productivity lever is a conversation lever. Conversations happen on the phone, at the desk, and in the service drive lane. The tools that track and support those conversations matter more to the fixed ops scorecard than the tools that track bay utilization.

## Why Voicemail Return Time Is the Service KPI Nobody Tracks

Talk to any GM running a [BDC](https://www.visquanta.com/blog/ai-bdc-guide-for-car-dealerships) the way we do and they'll tell you the same thing: almost no dealer tracks voicemail return time. Most service managers can quote their effective labor rate to two decimal places. Ask them the median time between a customer voicemail landing in the advisor queue and the return call going out, and you get a shrug. Tiffani names voicemail return inside one hour as one of the three behaviors that moves productivity more than any wrench-side incentive program ever did.

A dealer principal who wants to act on this Monday morning can do four concrete things:

- Pull yesterday's inbound service call log from the phone system and flag every voicemail
- Timestamp the return call for each one
- Calculate the median return time across the advisor team
- Post that number next to technician productivity on the service department board

The first week the number gets posted, it will be embarrassing. The second week, it will be better. By the fourth week, approved work hours are usually up. The technician productivity number climbs without anyone on the shop floor changing what they do.

Every voicemail sitting in the queue is a half-diagnosed RO sitting in a bay. The advisor team's responsiveness is the governor on the entire service department's output. Put the governor on the scorecard and the rest of the numbers follow. Leave it off, and the GM keeps blaming the shop for a problem that originates two steps upstream.

## Should Dealers Treat Service as a Phone Business First?

Talk to any GM running fixed ops the way we do and they'll tell you the same thing: the bigger takeaway from Tiffani's interview is a reorientation of what service actually is. Reframe the department as a communication and sales operation that happens to have a shop attached, and hours-per-tech tends to rise on its own. That's the sentence a dealer principal should pin to the service manager's wall. The shop is where the output shows up. The phones and the advisor desk are where it gets made.

For groups running multiple rooftops, the test is simple. Which store has the best technician productivity numbers quarter over quarter? Walk that store's service drive and watch the advisors. You'll find the team that answers the phone fastest, returns voicemails quickest, and spends the most time on the diagnostic walkthrough. The shop floor is not why that rooftop leads the group. The phones are.

The action item for a dealer principal reading this in 2026 is not to kill the wrench-side incentive program tomorrow. It is to stop treating technician productivity as the primary service KPI and start treating it as the lagging indicator it actually is. Measure approved work. Measure voicemail return time. Measure advisor phone pickup rate. The productivity number takes care of itself once the conversation at the front of the drive gets the attention it deserves.


## The Bottom Line

Fixed ops directors have spent thirty years pulling a lever that isn't connected to the machine. Technician productivity is the output; approved work sold at the advisor desk is the input, and no shop-floor spiff plan changes that order of operations.

**What this means for dealerships in 2026:**

- The 10 a.m. callback window is the single highest-leverage hour in the service department, not the 7:30 a.m. drop-off rush.
- Dealers measuring hours-per-tech without measuring advisor contact rate are flying with one instrument covered.
- A four-minute diagnostic walkthrough closes more approved labor than a three-minute price quote across 20 calls a day.
- Technician retention erodes when strong techs are punished for advisor-driven approval gaps they cannot control.
- The 2026 scorecard belongs to stores that measure approved work first and billed hours second.

The operators who move their attention from the bay to the advisor phone will find the productivity number follows on its own.


## Frequently Asked Questions

### Is technician productivity a useless KPI?

No, but it is a lagging indicator, not a leading one. It measures the output of the advisor-desk conversation, so using it as your primary scorecard means you are grading the wrong department for the result.

### What does it cost to shift from a tech-bonus model to an advisor-behavior model?

Essentially nothing in hard dollars. The three behaviors Tiffani names (live answer, one-hour callback, detailed walkthrough) require no software purchase or comp plan change, just a service manager redirecting attention from the back shop to the advisor desk.

### How long does it take to see the scoreboard move after refocusing on advisor behaviors?

Most rooftops see approved-hours-per-RO shift within two to four weeks of enforcing the 10 a.m. callback window and live-answer standard. Billed hours follow one pay period behind because they are downstream of approvals.

### What should I measure instead of, or alongside, billed hours per tech?

Track advisor live-answer rate on inbound, callback time on voicemails, and approved labor hours per RO written. Those three inputs feed the productivity number you already report, and they tell you where the pipeline is actually breaking.

### Why do my best technicians leave when productivity numbers drop?

Because they are being graded on approvals they cannot influence. When a strong tech watches their paycheck shrink because the advisor team left voicemails all morning, they find a store where the approval pipeline runs cleaner.

### Is the diagnostic walkthrough really worth the extra time per call?

Yes. Customers who understand why a repair matters approve it; customers who receive a clipped price quote defer. An advisor taking four extra minutes per call closes more approved labor than one cramming in three quick price quotes.

### Does this change anything about how I staff the service drive in 2026?

It changes who you coach and when. The highest-leverage coaching hour is the mid-morning callback window at the advisor desk, not the 7:30 a.m. drop-off rush or the end-of-day shop walkthrough.
