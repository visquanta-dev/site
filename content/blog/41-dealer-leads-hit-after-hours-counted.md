## What We Found Across 81 Dealerships in 30 Days

In 2026, we pulled 30 days of platform data across 81 rooftops and counted every customer conversion event that hit the stores. Inbound calls, texts, web form fills, and appointment requests all got logged together - from a buying-intent standpoint they are the same signal. The total came out to 12,609 events in a single month, per our internal platform data. That is the dataset this post is built on, and every number below traces back to it ([according to Visquanta](https://www.visquanta.com/internal/operator-seed/across-81-dealerships-on-our-platform-we-logged-12609-custom)).

The headline number: 41% of those 12,609 touchpoints landed outside the weekday 9am-to-6pm window in local time. That's not a rounding error, and it's not one outlier store dragging the average. The split held across all 81 rooftops in the same 30-day sample. Four in ten customers are raising their hand when the showroom lights are off. The 9-to-6 definition of a dealership's operating day is already behind the actual demand curve.

### The Scope of What Got Measured

The 81 dealerships in the sample were not cherry-picked. They represent a cross-section of franchise stores running on our [platform](https://www.visquanta.com/auto-master-suite) in Q1 2026. Every inbound call, text, form fill, and appointment request ran through the same logging pipeline - nothing double-counted, nothing estimated. When a customer raised their hand in any channel, the event got timestamped in the dealer's local timezone.

## When Are Customers Actually Reaching Out?

The 41% [after-hours](https://www.visquanta.com/blog/why-your-service-department-needs-24-7-call-answering-not-just-voicemail) figure breaks into three distinct windows, each with its own operational story. Per our platform data, 17.7% of touchpoints arrived on weekday evenings between 6pm and 11pm. Another 17.7% came in on weekends. And 6.4% landed overnight, between 11pm and 6am. Those three slices add up to the full 41%.

### Evenings Are as Big as Weekends

Notice the symmetry. Weekday evenings and weekends each carry the same 17.7% share of customer demand. Most dealers intuitively understand weekend volume - they [staff](https://www.visquanta.com/blog/ai-isnt-replacing-car-dealership-jobs-its-transforming-them) Saturdays. Far fewer treat Tuesday at 8pm as a peak buying moment. But the data says it is. A customer sitting on the couch after dinner is just as likely to reach out as one browsing on a Saturday afternoon.

### The Overnight Slice Is Small But Real

The 6.4% overnight window between 11pm and 6am is the smallest of the three. It is not negligible. On a 400-lead month, that slice represents roughly 25 real human beings who chose to ask about a vehicle in the middle of the night. They are often the highest-intent buyers in the set. Reaching out at 2am means the purchase decision has moved past idle browsing.

Stack that across a 30-day month and you get about 63 after-hours events per store, every month. All of it landing at hours when most dealerships have nobody at the desk to answer.

### The Real Cost in Dollars

Our research framed 2.1 per day as "nearly a full business-hours shift of volume, just distributed across hours most dealerships have nobody at the desk to answer." Think about that sentence in gross. ## Why Do Dealers Keep Staffing the Wrong Hours?

Having sold cars on the floor myself, the pattern we see across our rooftops is the staffing mismatch that fights decades of habit. Our data shows 41% of touchpoints hitting outside weekday 9am to 6pm, yet the standard dealership schedule still treats those hours as the center of gravity. Managers staff for when the showroom is physically busy, not for when the digital front door is ringing. Those two things used to be the same thing. In 2026, they are not.

### The Ghost Shift Problem

Per our platform data, 2.1 after-hours touchpoints per day represent "nearly a full business-hours shift of volume." No dealer would run their showroom with zero salespeople during a full shift. Yet that is exactly what happens to the digital channel every evening and every weekend night. A ghost shift of demand is coming in, with no corresponding ghost shift of coverage to meet it.

### Habit, Not Economics, Drives the Schedule

The real reason dealers staff 9 to 6 is not that it maximizes customer coverage. It is that the schedule was set before leads arrived through a phone held under a blanket at 11pm. The 41% figure is a 2026 reality, and it has grown every year as consumer research shifted online. The staffing plan has not moved with it.

## How Should a Dealer Respond to After-Hours Web Leads?

Catching the 41% does not require hiring a night shift. It requires being honest about which of those 12,609 monthly touchpoints across our 81-store sample actually need a human right now - and which just need a fast first response that holds the customer until morning. The ones that need the fastest answer are web form fills and [text message](https://www.visquanta.com/blog/5-reasons-car-buyers-prefer-sms-over-phone-calls-from-dealerships)s. Those are the channels where the customer is actively shopping other stores in the same session.

### A Practical After-Hours Playbook

Here is what a dealer can do this quarter to stop leaking the 41%:

1. Audit your own CRM for the same three windows we measured: weekday evenings (6pm-11pm), weekends, and overnight (11pm-6am).
2. Put an automated first-touch response on web form fills that fires in under 60 seconds, any hour, any day.
3. Route after-hours text messages to the same automated responder, not to a salesperson's personal phone that will not get checked.
4. Schedule one morning huddle item per week where the team [reviews](https://www.visquanta.com/reputation-management) every after-hours lead and what happened next.
5. Track set-rate on after-hours leads separately from business-hours leads. The two are different products.

### Match [Response Speed](https://www.visquanta.com/blog/speed-to-lead-why-it-matters-in-car-sales) to the Window

The three after-hours windows each deserve a different response: 17.7% evenings, 17.7% weekends, and 6.4% overnight. Evenings and weekends have high answer rates even on a callback the next morning, so an instant text acknowledgment plus a first-thing followup works. Overnight is trickier. The customer is often deciding in the moment, so automated responses need to handle the full back-and-forth, not just confirm receipt.

Both groups now get the same speed of first contact.
