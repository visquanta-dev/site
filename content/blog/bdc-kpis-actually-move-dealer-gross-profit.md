## Why Are Most Dealership BDCs Tracking the Wrong Numbers?

### The call-volume trap that hides poor BDC performance

Picture two [BDC](https://www.visquanta.com/blog/ai-bdc-guide-for-car-dealerships) managers standing in front of a dealer principal on Monday. One reports 1,000 calls handled last month at a 25% appointment rate. The other reports 600 calls at a 45% appointment rate. The first number sounds busier. The second number sells more cars. Volume metrics reward motion. Conversion metrics reward outcomes, and outcomes are what hit the F&I desk.

### What 'right metrics' actually means for a franchise dealer

Here is where the disconnect shows up on the P&L. Strolid's metrics glossary documents a 40 to 60 percent conversion lift in the first year for stores that track the right BDC numbers instead of raw call volume. Same [CRM](https://www.visquanta.com/blog/crm-database-reactivation-guide). Same staff. Same lead providers. The scoreboard changed, and the conversion rate followed it. For a franchise running 50 cars a month, even half of that lift is the difference between a flat quarter and a stair-step the manufacturer actually pays out on.


## What Is the Real Dollar Gap Between a 30% and 40% Appointment-to-Sale Rate?

### The $300K-$500K annual swing every GM should know

The figure that lands hardest in the dealer principal's office comes straight from Strolid's BDC research. A typical store that moves appointment-to-sale from 30 percent to 40 percent picks up $300,000 to $500,000 in annual gross profit. Lead count did not change. Floor plan did not change. Tier-three spend did not change. The only thing that moved was what the BDC did with the leads already sitting in the CRM, and that delta easily covers a second BDC manager with room left over.

### Translating five percentage points into monthly sales

You do not need a ten-point swing to feel it. Strolid's data shows dealerships that improve appointment set rate by just 10 percentage points typically see $40,000 to $60,000 in additional annual gross profit. That is one BDC rep's fully loaded cost recovered, with margin to spare. Five points of lift is roughly half that swing. Most stores have five points sitting in their aged-lead pile right now.

### How to present this math to ownership

Ownership does not buy percentage points. They buy units, gross, and bonus dollars. Strolid's metrics glossary lays out the translation a BDC manager should walk into the budget meeting with: a five-point lift on appointment set rate, from 22 to 27 percent, pencils out to roughly five more deliveries a month, about $15,000 in added front-end gross. Around $2,000 more in BDC bonus pay against the same [headcount](https://www.visquanta.com/blog/ai-isnt-replacing-car-dealership-jobs-its-transforming-them). Hand the dealer principal the unit number, the gross number, and the payroll number in one breath, and the BDC stops being a cost center on the next P&L review.


## How Fast Does Your BDC Actually Need to Respond to Web Leads?

### The 35-50% capture advantage of being first

Speed is the cheapest competitive advantage on the showroom floor. The 2023 [Cox Automotive](https://www.coxautoinc.com/) Lead Response Study found the first dealership to [contact](https://www.visquanta.com/book-demo) a web lead captures 35-50% of eventual sales. Price did not decide it. Inventory did not decide it. Whoever called or texted first walked away with the VIN. For a dealer group running multiple rooftops, this is the easiest gross to claim.

### What dropping response time from 20 to 5 minutes does to conversion

The bigger payoff is hiding in a smaller window. According to Strolid's 2026 BDC research, reducing lead [response time](https://www.visquanta.com/speed-to-lead) from 20 minutes to 5 minutes can lift conversion rates 30-40%. That is not a process redesign. It is fifteen minutes earlier on the same lead. Dealers who have not measured time-to-first-touch in their CRM are losing deals they never knew came in.

### After-hours leads: the 18-23% lift dealers leave on the table

Having sold cars on the floor myself, the pattern we see across our rooftops is that after-hours leads are where most dealerships quietly bleed. The 2024 Automotive BDC Performance Index reports dealerships using [24/7](https://www.visquanta.com/blog/why-your-service-department-needs-24-7-call-answering-not-just-voicemail) response automation see 18-23% higher conversion on evening and weekend leads. Web leads do not respect your BDC schedule. A lead that hits at 9:47 p.m. Friday and waits until Monday morning is a lead a competitor already worked.


## Which Appointment Metrics Separate Good BDCs From Great Ones?

### Appointment set rate benchmarks by lead source

Once you fix speed, the next question is whether your appointment set rate is good or just busy. Strolid's BDC research, citing the Automotive News BDC Impact Study from 2024, pegs the benchmark at 35 to 45 percent for stores running a mature BDC operation. A rooftop sitting at 22 percent is not running a mature BDC. It is running a phone room that books appointments only when the customer asks for one, and that gap shows up directly in monthly delivered units.

### Show rate targets for mature BDC operations

Show rate is the other half of the scoreboard. The 2023 Cox Automotive Dealership Operations Survey found the industry average is 40-50% for qualified appointments that actually show. A BDC booking 200 appointments at 35% show is delivering 70 ups. The same BDC at 50% show delivers 100 ups against the same booking effort. That is thirty extra at-bats for the sales floor.

## What Does a Healthy BDC Cost-Per-Sale Actually Look Like?

### Cost per appointment vs cost per sale

Cost per sale is the metric ownership actually wants to see. According to Strolid's BDC performance research, a dealer generating $2,500 gross profit per sale at a 30% appointment-to-sale rate is paying $45 to $75 to generate $750 in profit. That is a 10:1 return on every appointment dollar spent. Frame it that way and the conversation about BDC budget changes shape immediately.

### The 3.3:1 to 10:1 ROI range and what drives it

Scale the same math up to the whole department and the picture gets harder to argue with. Strolid's 2026 numbers put a typical BDC at $150,000 in monthly gross on a $45,000 all-in spend, assuming $2,500 average front-end gross per copy. Call it 3.3 dollars back for every dollar in, and that is after every payroll line, every CRM seat, and every phone bill is loaded against the department. The dealers who tell us their BDC is too expensive almost always have not pulled their own statement and run the division.

### Reading these numbers against your CRM

Strolid's metrics glossary walks through a worked example a GM can rebuild on a yellow pad before the Monday meeting. Load $30,000 of monthly BDC cost, including salaries, benefits, software, and overhead. Set the production at 50 BDC-attributed deliveries averaging $3,000 in gross. That is $150,000 in produced gross against $30,000 in spend, which runs (($150,000 minus $30,000) divided by $30,000) times 100 and lands at 400 percent [ROI](https://www.visquanta.com/blog/ai-roi-for-dealerships). Pull the same two numbers off your CRM this Friday. Whatever the answer is, that is the number ownership wants on the next budget review.


## How Does Appointment Velocity Quietly Kill Your Show Rate?

### The 1-3 day booking window top performers hit

Appointment velocity is the metric nobody puts on the daily standup, and every week the GM does not look at it, show rate quietly leaks. Strolid's BDC research, citing the Automotive BDC Velocity Study from 2023, pins the top-performer window at one to three days from first contact to a booked visit. Push the gap past five days and show rate drops about 40 percent against the same booking. A no-show that far out is not a customer who forgot. It is a customer the rooftop down the road already put on a delivery sheet.

### Why 5+ days correlates with 40% lower shows

Velocity is a function of contact rate, and contact rate is a function of how many times your BDC actually dials. CDK Global's external BDC data found that 45 to 50 percent of calls placed by a BDC generate an opportunity of some kind, in sales or service. That number is the ceiling your team is working against. If your floor is much lower, the issue is not script quality. It is dial discipline. [Research from Cdkglobal](https://www.cdkglobal.com/insights/3-metrics-measure-automotive-bdc-performance) backs the point.

### The 3.4 calls it takes to actually connect

The dial-count number every BDC manager should post on the wall comes from the same CDK research. It takes an average of 3.4 phone calls to connect with a customer. Reps who give up after one or two attempts are killing the appointment before it has a chance to be set. The math is simple. Three or four real attempts, spaced across hours and days, is the minimum to earn the conversation that earns the show.


## What Should a Dealer GM Review Every Week?

### The five numbers worth a Monday morning meeting

A dealer GM does not need fifteen daily KPIs. Five weekly numbers are enough if they are the right five. Strolid's 2026 ROI analysis shows that a $600 monthly technology investment that improves appointment rate by 5 percentage points generates $15,000 to $25,000 in additional annual gross profit. That is the kind of line item that earns its Monday morning slot, because the payback math fits on a sticky note.

### Translating percentages into dollars for ownership

Talk to any GM running a BDC the way we do and they'll tell you the same thing:. The numbers walk up to ownership, percentages lose every time and dollars win every time. Strolid's BDC metrics glossary gives dealers the script directly: "Our BDC delivers $120,000 net profit monthly on a $30,000 investment." That sentence does more in eleven seconds than a forty-slide deck. Ownership funds what they can repeat. Make the line repeatable.

### When to invest in technology vs more BDC headcount

The last weekly check is the denominator, and most stores never pull it. Strolid's 2026 research makes the point with simple arithmetic: a 20 percent set rate against 100 contacts is 20 appointments, while a 20 percent set rate against 500 contacts is 100 appointments. The scoreboard reads identical. The delivery log does not. Before the GM signs off on another seat or another tech stack line, pull contact volume next to set rate for the last ninety days. If volume is the weak number, more dials beat better dials. If volume is healthy and set rate is flat, then the script and the people are the conversation. Run that check first, and the budget decision picks itself.
